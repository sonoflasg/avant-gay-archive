**Zombie Formalism** is a (mostly pejorative) term for a market-driven wave of **abstract painting** that emerged in the early-to-mid 2010s. It was coined by critic [[Walter Robinson]] in a 2014 Artspace article and popularized by [[Jerry Saltz]] in New York Magazine, describing work that looked like “zombie” versions of earlier modernist abstraction (think echoes of [[Jackson Pollock]], [[Franz Kline]], [[Frank Stella]], or Color Field painting) — slick, process-oriented, visually striking, but often lacking deeper originality or conceptual bite.

### Key Characteristics
- **Aesthetic**: Gestural marks, pours, gradients, textures, and “cool” abstract compositions that felt fresh but safe for collectors. Easy to install, photogenic, and scalable.
- **Market Context**: Rose during a post-2008 art market boom. Galleries and collectors chased a new “trend” after previous waves. Many artists achieved rapid success with waiting lists and high auction prices, but it was criticized as market-manufactured rather than organic.
- **Criticism**: Seen as “undead” — reanimating past styles without true innovation, more about salability than substance. It became shorthand for cynical, trend-chasing abstraction.

### Parker Ito’s Connection
[[Parker Ito]] (CreamyDreamy) is frequently associated with Zombie Formalism, especially his early process-based abstractions, metallic pours, and post-internet experiments that aligned with the look. He has reflected on it as part of a market-driven moment parallel to post-internet art. Like many in that cohort ([[Lucien Smith]], [[Jacob Kassay]], etc.), he later moved away from it toward more personal, narrative, and digitally engaged work — including his deep involvement in the Solana [[Avant Gay ]]scene ([[Drilady]], [[Horses?2]] [[Heavy Liquid Graphic]], etc.).

In short, Zombie Formalism was a short-lived but influential “movement” defined more by market dynamics than a unified ideology. Parker and others who outgrew it often cite it as a phase that taught them about hype cycles while they evolved into new directions (like the [[trait-maxxing]] and collage energy of the current scene).
